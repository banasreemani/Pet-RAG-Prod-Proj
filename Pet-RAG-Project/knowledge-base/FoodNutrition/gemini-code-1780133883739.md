### Abstract
Many common human foods trigger severe metabolic failure in dogs and cats. This article details the specific toxic mechanisms and internal damage caused by these foods.

### Toxicity Matrix

| Toxic Agent | Source Foods | Primary Mechanism | Clinical Presentation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Methylxanthines** (Theobromine, Caffeine) | Chocolate, coffee | Inhibits cellular phosphodiesterase; causes intracellular calcium accumulation. | Tachycardia, cardiac arrhythmias, muscle tremors, seizures. |
| **Thiosulfate / Organosulfoxides** | Allium species (Onions, garlic, leeks) | Triggers oxidative damage to red blood cells, causing Heinz body formation. | Hemolytic anemia, pale mucous membranes, dark or discolored urine. |
| **Xylitol** | Sugar-free gum, peanut butter | Causes a rapid, massive spike in insulin release from the pancreas. | Severe hypoglycemia, acute hepatic necrosis (liver failure). |
| **Tartaric Acid** | Grapes, raisins | Causes targeted necrosis of the renal proximal tubules. | Acute kidney injury, anuria (inability to produce urine). |

### Triage Thresholds
If an animal ingests any of these compounds, contact emergency veterinary services immediately. If the ingestion occurred within the last two hours and the animal is fully conscious, emesis (inducing vomiting) may be advised, followed by the administration of activated charcoal to bind remaining toxins.
