### Abstract
Regular ungual (nail) maintenance prevents structural changes in an animal's gait and mitigates the risk of painful dewclaw ingrowth. This document covers the internal anatomy of the claw and emergency protocols for quick vascular ruptures.

### Anatomical Mapping
The canine and feline claw consists of a hard outer sheath of keratin surrounding the "quick"—a highly vascularized, sensitive core containing the dorsal dermal papilla and digital nerve endings. 

### Execution Protocol
1. **Identification:** In translucent nails, locate the pink-tinted quick line. In pigmented (black) nails, look for the distal ventral groove; trim in small increments parallel to the sole of the claw.
2. **Cutting Action:** Position the trimmer (guillotine or scissor-style) at a 45-degree angle just distal to the quick. A clean, rapid compression prevents crushing fractures of the keratin matrix.
3. **Internal Indicator:** When trimming dark nails, stop when the cut surface reveals a gray, fleshy circle surrounded by white keratin; this signifies you are within 1–2 millimeters of live tissue.

### Hemostasis
If the quick is accidentally cut, apply heavy pressure using styptic powder (ferric subsulfate). This acts as a chemical cauterization agent, rapidly coagulating proteins to arrest bleeding.
